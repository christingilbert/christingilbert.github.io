Devotion Can Be Wordless

Kumano Kodo, Nakahechi Route, Japan · Pilgrimage Japan Place Travel

The first day out of Takijiri-oji rose almost seven hundred metres straight up. Twenty-six pounds on my shoulders. The weight felt literal, maybe it wasn't. Nothing but endless moss-covered stone steps and exposed ancient roots twisting across the trail. There were still fifty-some kilometres ahead.

I once imagined a few plans for this Japan trip. Instead, I started the Kumano Kodo hike with leftover fever from a cold. Rain and strong wind from Typhoon Nakri's outer bands blurred the forest and the Kii mountain range. Somewhere between wiping rain off my face, the plan changed shape and the grand plan faded. I found myself surrounded by quiet days and conserving warmth.

Their resinous scent, faintly medicinal with a trace of sweetness, was deeply comforting and clearing my thoughts.

Roots and rocks, slippery up and slippery down, while small red river crabs crossed the trail and giant hornets drifted by, uninterested. The path wound through dense cedar, camphor and cypress trees.

I placed one more pebble on top of the wooden sign, like the other pilgrims. A small act of gratitude that these tired feet had carried me across the Nakahechi. Beyond Koguchi, the sense of distance dissolved into repetition. Twelve hundred metres of ascent yet no summit to conquer, only long stretches of rain-slick stone steps, mud and roots to move through.

At Nachi Taisha, my muddy boots were a reminder that devotion can be wordless.

I wonder how in the end I could feel so weightless yet so awake. Maybe that's the clearest view there is. The humbling sensation of not being the centre, and finding peace in that.
