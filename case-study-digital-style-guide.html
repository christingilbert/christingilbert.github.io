The Situation

As digital channels expanded across web, email, and social media, the number of visual assets required for campaigns increased significantly. The brand guidelines defined the company's identity at a high level, but they did not translate directly into practical rules for digital production. Designers and marketers interpreted the brand in slightly different ways across platforms, which led to inconsistencies in composition, imagery, and content structure.

At the same time, the team needed to produce a growing volume of digital assets while maintaining brand integrity and accessibility standards. The challenge was not simply design quality. It was creating a system that could make digital content consistent, scalable, and accessible across the organization.

The Challenge

Brand guidelines often describe what a brand represents, but digital production requires clear rules for how the brand should appear in real interfaces and campaign assets. Without structured guidance, designers interpreted visual direction differently across projects, campaign assets varied in composition and hierarchy, and accessibility considerations were applied inconsistently.

The organization needed a framework that could translate brand identity into repeatable visual rules for digital production.

New team members required time to learn informal practices that weren't documented anywhere. The goal was to replace that informal knowledge with a shared system anyone could use.

The Approach

The Digital Visual Style Guide was designed to serve as a bridge between brand guidelines and design systems, translating brand values into practical rules for content production. The system was built around five principles: consistency, brand alignment, accessibility, scalability, and production efficiency.

The guide established both creative direction and technical specifications, allowing designers, marketers, and production teams to work from a shared visual framework.

Principle 01
Sensory Experience
Visuals emphasize tactile textures and spa-like environments to communicate calmness and wellness.
Principle 02
Simplicity & Minimalism
Clean compositions and generous negative space allow products to remain the visual focus.
Principle 03
Organic & Natural Elements
Natural materials such as wood, stone, leaves, and fabrics reinforce the brand's connection to organic ingredients.
Principle 04
Emotion-Driven Imagery
Scenes emphasize mindfulness, relaxation, and personal care moments, with diverse representation across skin tones, ages, and gender expressions.

Together, these principles created a coherent emotional and visual identity across digital experiences — giving teams a shared vocabulary for creative decisions rather than individual interpretation.

Website Structure

Beyond creative direction, the guide defined the visual architecture of the website. Homepage hero banners were standardized with layout rules that defined safe zones for text overlays and responsive behavior across desktop and mobile, ensuring promotional content remained readable and visually balanced regardless of screen size.

The guide also established a mobile-first approach, with clear specifications for text placement, CTA visibility, and cropping safety zones. The Product Detail Page system organized pages into modular content blocks, each with defined specs and accessibility requirements.

  • Product Hero Images Aspect ratio, resolution, file size limits, and sRGB color profile requirements for consistent rendering across devices.
  • Texture & Close-Up Swatches Composition guidance for detail shots communicating ingredient quality and tactile experience.
  • Demonstration Videos Aspect ratio, resolution, and burned-in subtitle requirements with defined minimum font sizes and safe zone placement.
  • Lifestyle Imagery Composition rules and emotional register guidance per block type, distinguishing functional trust imagery from connection-driven storytelling.
  • Social-Style Vertical Video Specs for short-form vertical content reused across PDP and social channels, with caption overlay and safe zone requirements.

This modular structure allowed pages to scale from simple layouts to richer storytelling formats while maintaining visual consistency across the site.

Accessibility & Digital Standards

Accessibility was integrated directly into the visual system. Rather than a separate compliance section, each content block included its own alt text structure, contrast standards, and placement requirements — making inclusive design a built-in part of following the standard.

Recommendations included alt text descriptions for images and video, readable text overlays, contrast standards for captions, mobile-safe content placement, and clear product visibility within images. These practices ensured the website experience remained inclusive and aligned with accessibility expectations.

Outcomes
Instead of reinventing layouts and image structures for every campaign, teams could work within a clear framework that balanced creativity with consistency.
  • Digital content aligned with brand identity across marketing, design, and production teams
  • Reduced inconsistencies across campaigns through shared visual rules
  • Faster asset creation through standardized formats and modular page structures
  • Improved collaboration between designers and marketers working from a common reference
  • Accessibility practices embedded directly into production workflows at the component level
  • A scalable system for digital visual production that grows with the organization

Design systems are often associated with UI components, but visual content systems are equally important. By translating brand identity into structured digital rules, the style guide helped the organization move from individual creative interpretation to shared creative infrastructure — a system that holds its standards without needing someone in the room to enforce them.
