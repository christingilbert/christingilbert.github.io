The Situation

As demand for digital content grew across websites, email and social channels, the gap between brand guidelines and day-to-day production became a real problem. Brand guidelines described the identity — the values, the voice, the visual principles. But they didn't tell a designer exactly how to crop a hero banner for mobile, or how much contrast an overlay caption needed to be accessible, or what aspect ratio to use for a demo video on a PDP page.

The result was inconsistency. Designers made different calls on the same decisions. New team members had no shared reference to onboard from. Cross-functional teams — design, marketing, production — were working from different assumptions about what "on brand" meant in practice.

From the Digital Visual Style Guide — internal documentation developed for the digital creative team.

The Challenge

Brand guidelines describe what a brand stands for. But digital production needs something more specific — rules about how that brand should actually look in a homepage carousel, a product detail page, a social post or an email header. Without that translation layer, every project became a fresh interpretation. Layouts varied. Accessibility criteria were applied inconsistently. Onboarding new team members was slow because the knowledge lived in people's heads, not in a shared document.

The goal was to build that translation layer — a system that could carry brand identity into digital production reliably, at scale, across teams that didn't always work in the same room.

The Approach

I developed the Digital Visual Style Guide as the bridge between brand guidelines and design systems — a living document that translates brand philosophy into actionable rules for content production. The system was built around five principles: consistency, brand alignment, accessibility, scalability and production efficiency.

Principle 01
Sensory Experience
Visuals emphasize tactile textures and spa-like environments to communicate calmness and wellness.
Principle 02
Simplicity & Minimalism
Clean compositions and generous negative space allow products to remain the visual focus.
Principle 03
Organic & Natural Elements
Natural materials such as wood, stone, leaves and fabrics reinforce the brand's connection to organic ingredients.
Principle 04
Emotion-Driven Imagery
Scenes emphasize mindfulness, relaxation and personal care moments, with diverse representation across skin tones, ages and gender expressions.

These principles gave the teams a shared vocabulary for creative decisions rather than individual interpretation.

Website Structure

Besides setting the mood and tone of the visual, the guide also determined the visual structure of the website. The hero banners on the homepage followed layout specifications defining safe zones for headlines and text overlays, responsiveness on desktop and mobile, allowing visual assets to be legible across screen sizes.

The guide also established a mobile-first approach, with clear specifications for text placement, CTA visibility and cropping safety zones. Hero banner specifications were defined across four breakpoints — 1280px, 1440px, 1680px and 1920px — with safe zones, text box placement and CTA visibility documented for both still and video carousel formats.

The Product Detail Page (PDP) system structured how pages were divided into separate content blocks, each with fixed specifications for content type, aspect ratio and accessibility requirements.

I developed a modular visual style guide in Figma covering web, social media, email and video content. The system was built as a library of reusable components, allowing teams to assemble layouts by selecting the modules needed for each platform while maintaining visual consistency across channels.

Accessibility & Digital Standards

Accessibility was integrated into the visual system. Each content module included defined alt text guidance, contrast requirements, and placement rules, embedding inclusive design standards directly into the production framework.

As part of the web accessibility recommendations, the use of alt text for images and video, legible font for overlays, caption contrast standards, mobile-friendly position of content, and easy-to-identify products within the image were to be followed. These would help ensure the website remained an accessible tool for users and that the content was inclusive.

Outcomes
Instead of reinventing layouts and image structures for every campaign, cross-functional teams could work within a clear framework that balanced creativity with consistency.
  • Digital content aligned with brand identity across marketing, design, and production teams
  • Reduced inconsistencies across campaigns through shared visual rules
  • Faster asset creation through standardized formats and modular page structures
  • Improved collaboration between designers and marketers working from a common reference
  • Accessibility practices embedded directly into production workflows at the component level
  • A scalable system for digital visual production that grows with the organization

Design systems are often viewed only through the lens of UI components, but the visual content system is equally important. The style guide translated brand identity into digital rules that are consistently maintained and kept relevant. This allowed the organization to shift from individual creative interpretation to collective creative standard.
