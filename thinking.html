Notes on Creative Practice

Building creative systems, with care.

I often think about this question when dealing with problems: how can creative systems increase production while still being careful, considerate, and responsible towards the people and places represented?

As automated systems take over creative production, creativists might focus more on curating. They will design experiences that allow people to engage with creative work in a deeper way.

01 Creative Work

The invisible layer behind visible work

Building the operational structures that support creative work.

From what I've seen, creative work is usually judged by what is visible, like images, campaigns, videos, and stories that people see. If there isn't a good system in place, teams keep facing the same problems repeatedly. They spend time looking for materials, explaining design rules, or setting up the production process for every new campaign.

"Behind every piece of creative work is an invisible foundation that makes it possible."

The invisible layer behind visible work.

Accessibility is usually the first thing to be neglected in this situation. Instead of being planned from the start, it becomes an afterthought that often vanishes when deadlines approach.

In my own work, I've seen that using modular visual style guides and production frameworks tends to be more impactful over time than just single campaigns. These tools create an environment that lets creative teams concentrate on important projects while the framework ensures consistency and efficiency without much notice. If we include accessibility in our workflows and style guides right from the beginning, it becomes a normal part of the process rather than something we fix at the end.

02 Creative Technology

From Experimentation to Responsible Use

Integrating AI into creative production with intention, standards and accountability.

Generative AI offers exciting new opportunities for creating content. However, to go from trying things out to consistently producing results, it's not enough to just use new tools. AI becomes really helpful when it's part of an organized and carefully planned work process.

AI adoption moves through three stages. Most teams stall at Stage 01. Governance is what makes it reliable.

AI adoption often follows certain steps. Many teams first try out the technology in an unstructured way. Setting up rules and guidelines helps make the technology dependable and able to grow.

Generative AI shouldn't just be considered a new technology; it should be viewed as a tool that needs clear guidelines to be used responsibly.

Accessibility can be a real issue with AI-created content. Images may not have useful alt text, and their contrast or clarity might not follow brand rules. These factors are important to make sure the content is available to all intended audience.

Governance is most effective when it is planned from the start. By including accessibility standards, keeping records, and reviewing in the workflow, responsible use becomes part of the system. The main challenge is creating the structures for AI-assisted production to work responsibly within a creative environment.

03 Creative Responsibility

Documenting Place Beyond the Image

Writing documentation while considering the background, ensuring it is easy for everyone to access, and showing care for the environments and people involved.

Photographs and storytelling through images have always influenced how we see and grasp the idea of places. Pictures can show us beauty, cultural history, and how people relate to their environment. However, they can also turn places into something to simply look at.

Stewardship and Accountability
Two ways of approaching the same landscape, each shaped by intention.

This tension is clear in places where tourism, work, and nature all come together. A view that visitors find beautiful might be part of the everyday job, danger or earnings for the locals.

Documentation is mainly about providing access. For creative teams, it gives clear information. This helps team members understand the systems, share them, and use them independently as the work grows. For landscapes and cultural sites, documentation allows people to experience these places even if they never go there physically.

Being thorough in documenting helps to keep the memories and importance of places alive. This allows future generations to keep understanding and valuing these places in the future.
