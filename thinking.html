01 Creative Work

The invisible layer behind visible work

Building the operational structures that support creative work.

Creative work is usually judged by what people can see: the images, campaigns, videos, and visual storytelling that reach the public. But behind every successful piece of creative work is a structure that quietly makes it possible.

The invisible layer behind visible work.

Without that infrastructure, teams often find themselves solving the same operational problems again and again: searching for assets, clarifying visual standards, or rebuilding production processes for every new campaign. Accessibility suffers most in this environment. It becomes a last-minute check rather than a built-in standard, consistently the first thing dropped when deadlines compress.

In my own work, building modular visual style guides and production frameworks has often had a longer-lasting impact than any individual campaign. These systems create the conditions that allow creative teams to focus on what they do best: making thoughtful, meaningful visual work, while the structure quietly supports consistency and efficiency.

Accessibility is part of this infrastructure too. When it is built into production workflows and style guides from the start, it becomes a standard rather than a late-stage fix, something the system holds rather than something individuals have to remember.

Creative infrastructure is rarely visible, but when it's missing, everyone feels it.

02 Creative Technology

From Experimentation to Responsible Use

Integrating AI into creative production with intention, standards and accountability.

Generative AI has opened up remarkable new possibilities in creative production. But moving from experimentation to reliable production requires more than simply adopting new tools. In practice, AI becomes truly useful when it is integrated into a clear and thoughtful workflow.

Stage 01

Stage 03
Govern
  • Style guide integration
  • AI vs. traditional tagging
  • Accessibility standards
  • Responsible use policy
  • Sustainable at scale
AI adoption moves through three stages. Most teams stall at Stage 01. Governance is what makes it reliable.

This is why I tend to think about AI adoption less in terms of novelty and more in terms of governance. Accessibility is a particular risk with AI-generated content. Generated imagery often lacks meaningful alt text. Contrast and legibility can drift outside brand standards. These are not small details. They determine whether the content reaches everyone it is intended for. Governance structures need to account for this from the start, not as a checklist at the end.

The real challenge isn't generating images. It's building systems that allow AI-assisted production to operate responsibly within a creative ecosystem.

03 Creative Responsibility

Documenting Place Beyond the Image

Approaching documentation with context, accessibility and care for the landscapes and communities involved.

Photography and visual storytelling have long shaped the way we experience and understand places. Images can reveal beauty, cultural memory, and the layered relationship between people and landscape. But they can also simplify places into spectacle.

Two ways of approaching the same landscape, each shaped by intention.

Documentation is ultimately about access. For creative teams, documentation provides clarity. It allows systems to be understood, shared, and used independently as work scales across teams and campaigns.

For landscapes and cultural environments, documentation can extend access beyond physical presence. Through photography, spatial capture, or other forms of recording, places can be experienced by people who may never physically visit them.

In the end, thoughtful documentation helps preserve the memory and meaning of places so they can continue to be understood and appreciated over time.
