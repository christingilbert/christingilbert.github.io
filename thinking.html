Notes on Creative Practice

Building creative systems, with care.

I often return to this question: how can creative systems scale production while remaining considerate and responsible toward the people and places they represent?

As automated systems take on more of production, the creative roles may gradually shift toward curation, shaping experiences that allow people to engage with the work in more meaningful ways.

01 Creative Work

The invisible layer behind visible work

Building the operational structures that support creative work.

From what I've seen and experienced, creative work is often judged by what is visible: the images, campaigns, videos and stories people experience. Without a strong system in place, teams end up solving the same problems repeatedly: searching for materials, re-explaining design rules or rebuilding production processes for each new campaign.

Behind every piece of creative work is an invisible foundation that makes it possible.

Accessibility is often the first thing to be overlooked. When it isn't planned from the start, it becomes an afterthought that can easily disappear when deadlines are near. In this context, accessibility means who is able to use and work inside the system.

In my experience, I've noticed that using modular style guides and production frameworks are more impactful over time. Many teams already have some kind of guidelines or documentation, but these are often scattered across different tools and teams, not clearly defined, or not updated as workflows change.

When these frameworks are carefully designed, they allow teams to concentrate on important work, while the system consistently runs in the background. If accessibility is included in workflows and style guides from the start, it naturally becomes part of the process, making both the work and the system easier to understand.

02 Creative Technology

From Experimentation to Responsible Use

Integrating AI as a tool into creative production with intention and accountability.

AI is becoming part of modern creative production, with generative tools enabling new ways of creating images, video and text. I've found that these tools become more valuable when they produce consistent results, which comes from integrating AI into workflows and making it a standard part of how work is produced.

AI adoption moves through three stages. Most teams stall at Stage 01. Governance is what makes it reliable.

As workflows mature, parts of the process can be automated, reducing manual coordination and allowing teams to focus on more meaningful work. Automation creates consistency and makes it easier to trace things over time.

Consistency without intention becomes repetition.

This is where governance comes in, giving structure a clear role and purpose. The real challenge is building structures that allow AI-assisted production to operate responsibly within a creative environment, where consistency and creative flexibility can coexist.

03 Creative Responsibility

Where Systems Shape How We See

Creative systems and technologies shape how we create work and how we represent and understand places, people and culture.

Yet scaling production can create distance. As systems move faster, there are more opportunities to lose context: a place becomes a setting, a person becomes a detail, and culture is reduced to aesthetics. This is rarely intentional; it is often the result of efficiency becoming the primary measure of success.

  • Place becomes a setting

Why
Intention
When care is the measure
  • Context preserved alongside the work
  • People named, not implied
  • Culture held with specificity
  • Decisions trace back to a person
  • Consistency earned, not assumed
  • Who we are designing for, asked early
  • Who is included, part of the process
Care as a design decision
What systems optimise for shapes what they leave behind.

In my experience, the difference rarely comes from more resources or more time. It comes from intention: who we are designing for, and who is included in the process.

Care, in this context, is a design decision. It means preserving meaning within the work.

The work that lasts usually begins with that decision made early, and someone who held it all the way through.
