Overview

A site where disaster, memory and meaning meet

Type
Independent Documentation Project
Status

Mount Merapi is an active volcano in Central Java, southern part of Yogyakarta, and is the most active of all volcanoes. The name Merapi literally means "Fire Mountain" in the Javanese language. It erupted consistently since the year 1548. Most recently, in 2010, the volcano erupted, resulting in the relocation of over 300,000 people, killing 353 individuals. The volcano erupted again in 2018, then in 2021, and in 2023.

Museum Gunung Merapi, is educational and society memorial site to preserve and display the history of Merapi eruption, to interpret and provide context to the communities surrounding it. The museum has collections of the eruption in 2010, seismographic tools, geological maps, and the community story of disaster and rehabilitation.

For Javanese people, Merapi is more than just a volcano. It's a presence in the cultural cosmology, in the myths, in the daily life. The museum embodies this duality: scientific record and cultural memory, disaster statistics and human experience.

The Question

How do you document a place built to preserve memory?

Museum Gunung Merapi first and foremost is a place for memory and for remembering and preserving. The question this project seeks to explore is how spatial documentation can benefit the museum's cause further, and if processes such as 3D capture and structured photography can help the museum's outreach beyond its physical boundaries.

Physical museums are limited by their geography, accessibility, and the limited lifespan of the objects we might collect. One day someone in Vancouver, a researcher in Jakarta, or even a member of the displaced community due to the eruption in the future may be unable to access the Museum in-person.

Spatial documentation creates a travelling record, that can be accessed remotely, and that is potentially usable forever beyond the potential deterioration of the original objects.
Approach

Documentation with context and care

The project is guided by a set of principles that inform both what is documented and how it is framed.

  • 01 Cultural environment over tourist attraction. The museum as a cultural environment, not a disastrous place. That is the primary concern of this approach. The objects, spaces, and records will be photographed and documented only to the extent that their context communicates their significance — not just their visual impact.
  • 02 Curate by locals. The museum is created and curated by people. The documentation will acknowledge the effort of local communities to conserve the collection, rendering the space beyond the physical museum.
  • 03 Rely on the community voice wherever possible. These documents will promote the use of local insight and place based knowledge to represent the community perspective rather than the imposition of an outsider's view of the site.
  • 04 Access by design. The finished product will be designed to be easily accessible and useable by both in-person and remote audiences.
Personal Context

Why this site

My study of the Indonesian history and heritage brings me that is rooted on the island of Java, where I learned about the Javanese cosmology and its integral question of how the landscape relates to the people. I choose to engage with the museum personally, as someone who has connection, both genealogically and culturally, on how it presents Indonesian histories.

It creates a parallel record that allows the place to be understood by wider audience over a longer time.
